STD 5 · CHAPTER 1

Computer and Its Evolution

History of computing, early calculating machines, and the five generations of computers.

Lesson Plan Explanation Generations Quick Check Exercise Solutions Extra MCQs Oral Viva Activity Zone Lab Activity Homework Blackboard Revision Notes

Quick Lesson Plan

Duration: 40 minutes
Topics: History of Computing · Early Calculating Machines · Computer Generations

Objectives: students should be able to name early calculating devices and their inventors, explain why each computer generation improved on the last, and recall who is called the "Father of the Computer."

Hook / Introduction (5 min)

Ask the class: "How did people add big numbers before calculators existed?" Let a few students guess (fingers, sticks, counting on paper), then say: "Today we will travel back in time and meet the machines that came before the computer."

Explain in Simple Words

What does "computing" mean?

The word computing simply means calculating. Long before electronic computers existed, people used pen and paper, chalk and slate, or simple tools to calculate.

1. Abacus

The oldest known calculating tool — a wooden frame with rods and beads. Used in Europe, China and Russia long before our modern number system became common.

Simple exampleIt's like an old-fashioned, hand-operated calculator made of beads.

2. Napier's Bones

A set of rod-like strips, originally made from bone/ivory. Invented by John Napier, a Scottish mathematician. Used mainly for multiplication and division.

Simple exampleImagine sliding number-strips next to each other instead of multiplying in your head.

3. Pascaline

Invented by Blaise Pascal, a French mathematician, and patented in 1647. The first mechanical device that could add and subtract whole numbers on its own.

Simple exampleIt's the great-grandparent of today's calculator.

4. Punched Card System

Invented by Herman Hollerith, an American statistician who worked for the US Census Bureau. Used to store and retrieve data — the data was coded as a pattern of punched holes in a card.

Simple exampleLike a ticket with holes punched in specific places that a machine could "read."

5. Difference Engine

Invented by Charles Babbage in 1822. An automatic mechanical calculator built to produce error-free mathematical tables.

6. Analytical Engine

Also designed by Charles Babbage, in 1837 — an improved version of the Difference Engine. Considered the first general-purpose computer because it had an arithmetic unit, the ability to make decisions (branching) and loops, and a memory — the same basic parts (input, processing, memory, output) found in computers today.

Important factCharles Babbage is known as the "Father of the Computer."

Fun fact for the board: the first full-sized digital computer, called the Mark I, was built in 1944 and was used only for calculations — it weighed about five tons!

The Five Computer Generations

GenerationYearsMain TechnologyKey Points
1st1946–1956Vacuum TubesHuge in size, slow, used a lot of power, generated heat, very little memory, used machine language
2nd1956–1963TransistorsSmaller & faster (made of silicon), used magnetic tape/disks for memory, programmed in assembly language, less heat
3rd1965–1971Integrated Circuits (IC) — Jack Kilby & Robert NoyceSmall chip combining transistor + resistor + capacitor; keyboard & monitor used for input/output
4th1971–1980Microprocessor (Intel 4004, made with LSI/VLSI)Personal computers began; high-level languages; cheaper & more reliable
5thSince 1980Artificial IntelligenceUnderstands natural language, recognises images, helps with decisions; involves quantum computing & nanotechnology

Memory trick: Vacuum Tube → Transistor → Integrated Circuit → Microprocessor → Artificial Intelligence

STD 6 · CHAPTER 1

More About Computers

Types of computer, computer language, and the characteristics and limitations of a computer.

Lesson Plan Types of Computer Computer Language Characteristics Limitations Quick Check Exercise Solutions Extra MCQs Oral Viva Activity Zone Homework Blackboard Revision Notes

Quick Lesson Plan

Duration: 40 minutes
Topics: Types of Computer · Computer Language · Characteristics · Limitations

Objectives: students should be able to classify computers by size/power, explain the difference between Low-Level and High-Level Language, and list the characteristics and limitations of a computer with examples.

Hook / Introduction (5 min)

Ask: "Can a computer think like a human, on its own?" Let students answer (most will say "No"). Then explain: "A computer is super-fast and accurate, but it only does exactly what we tell it to do — it has no mind of its own."

Types of Computer

Depending on size and processing power, computers are grouped into four broad categories.

1. Supercomputers

The most powerful kind; can perform an enormous number of calculations every second. Used for jobs like modelling molecules, weather forecasting, nuclear research, and analysing geological data.

2. Mainframe Computers

Big, powerful machines used by large organisations — banks, government departments, big companies — that need to store huge amounts of information.

Board noteSupercomputer speed is measured in FLOPS (Floating Point Operations Per Second); mainframe power is measured in MIPS (Millions of Instructions Per Second).

3. Minicomputers

Smaller than a mainframe but can still support around 200 users at once. About the size of a refrigerator; many servers are minicomputers (though not every server is one).

4. Microcomputers

Built around a microprocessor; much cheaper than the above three — the category most of us use every day. Divided into:

  • Home Computers — for everyday household use.
  • Laptop Computers — compact, light, made for travelling.
  • Workstations — a powerful single-user computer with a high-quality monitor.
  • Personal Computer (PC) — smaller, less powerful, inexpensive single-user computer (Desktops, Notebooks, Palmtops, PDAs).
  • Smartphone — a pocket-sized computer combining calling and computing (e.g., attending online classes or video calls on a phone).

Computer Language

Just like people need a common language to talk to each other, a computer needs a language to receive our instructions.

  • Low-Level Language (LLL) / Machine Language — made only of 0s and 1s; the computer's native, "raw" language.
  • High-Level Language (HLL) — closer to human language, easier for people to read and write.

Characteristics of Computer

  • Speed — does in seconds what might take a person hours (e.g., adding 1,000 numbers).
  • Accuracy — gives the same correct result every time, as long as the data fed in is correct.
  • Diligence — never gets bored or tired; can work for hours without a single mistake.
  • Versatility — can switch between totally different jobs (typing a letter one moment, playing music the next).
  • Power of Remembering — can store information for as long as needed and recall it instantly.
  • Storage — has temporary (built-in) memory that is lost when switched off, plus permanent secondary storage (hard disk, pen drive, CD/DVD) that keeps data safe and portable.

Limitations of Computer

  • No IQ — cannot think for itself; if the user gives wrong data or a wrong instruction, it cannot catch the mistake — it will simply produce a wrong answer.
  • No Feelings — being a machine, it has no emotions, taste, or real-life experience, and treats every user exactly the same way.

STD 7 · CHAPTER 1

Computer System in Detail

Data processing, hardware, every input and output device, and the CPU's three units.

Lesson Plan Data Processing Input Devices CPU Output Devices Quick Check Exercise Solutions Extra MCQs Oral Viva Activity Zone Lab Activity Homework Blackboard Revision Notes

Quick Lesson Plan

Duration: 40 minutes
Topics: Data Processing · Hardware · Input Devices · CPU · Output Devices

Objectives: students should be able to identify input and output devices, describe the CPU's three units, and name the main parts found inside the CPU cabinet.

Hook / Introduction (5 min)

Show (or describe) a desktop computer setup. Ask: "What parts can you see, and what do you think each one does?"

Computer System & Data Processing

Computer System: a collection of parts designed to receive, process, manage, and present information in a useful form.

Data Processing is a simple flow: Data → Input → Process → Output → Information (with Storage connected at every stage). In simple words: we feed raw data in, the computer processes it, and useful information comes out — and at every stage, data can be stored for later use.

Computer Hardware means the physical, touchable parts of a computer that support input, processing, output, storage and communication.

Input Devices

Anything used to enter data or instructions into the computer:

  • Keyboard — keys grouped as alphabet keys, number keys, function keys, special keys.
  • Mouse — pointing device with two buttons and a scroll wheel; moves the cursor.
  • Joystick — used to control movement in computer games.
  • Microphone — records sound (an audio input device).
  • Scanner — optically scans printed pages/photos/objects and turns them into a digital image.
  • Barcode Reader — reads the barcode printed on products (seen at shopping mall checkout counters).
  • Webcam — captures photos and videos; used for video chatting.
  • Touchpad — works like a mouse; moving a finger on it moves the pointer.
  • Touchscreen — a screen that responds directly to a finger's touch.
  • Light Pen — pen-like device used to point at, select, or draw directly on the screen.
  • MICR (Magnetic Ink Character Recognition) — special magnetic ink used to print characters on documents like cheques, to verify they are genuine.
  • OMR (Optical Mark Recognition/Reading) — reads marks filled by hand on forms such as multiple-choice answer sheets.

CPU — the "Brain" of the Computer

The CPU is the main cabinet to which all the computer's parts are connected by cables. It has three units:

  • CU (Control Unit) — directs the operations.
  • ALU (Arithmetic Logic Unit) — does calculations and comparisons.
  • MMU (Main Memory Unit) — holds data and instructions currently in use.

Output from the CPU goes either as Softcopy (e.g., shown on a Monitor) or Hardcopy (e.g., printed by a Printer or a Plotter).

Inside the Cabinet

  • Motherboard — the main circuit board; connects and allows communication between the CPU, RAM and other parts, and supplies them power.
  • Microprocessor — the chip that does the CPU's job on a single integrated circuit.
  • Power Supply — provides power to all internal parts.
  • Hard Drive — stores the operating system, programmes, and files like documents, photos and music.
  • RAM (Random Access Memory) — the computer's internal, working memory.

Output Devices

Monitor

The main output device. Three common types:

  • CRT (Cathode Ray Tube) — old, bulky type.
  • LCD (Liquid Crystal Display) — flat-screen type.
  • LED (Light-Emitting Diode) — flat-screen type with better picture quality than LCD.

Printer

Gives a hard copy (printed paper). Types include:

  • Dot Matrix — forms letters from a grid (matrix) of small dots.
  • Daisy-wheel — uses a wheel of pre-formed letter shapes for high-quality print (older technology, from 1969).
  • Line Printer — prints one full line at a time.
  • Drum Printer — an old wide-format printer where characters form around a rotating drum.
  • Chain Printer — an early printer where linked type-pieces on a moving chain strike the ribbon and paper.
  • Band Printer — similar idea, but uses a band/loop of type characters instead of a chain.
  • Inkjet Printer — sprays tiny drops of ink onto paper.
  • Laserjet Printer — gives high-quality text and graphics using a laser.
  • Multifunction Printer — combines print, scan, copy, fax and email in one machine.
  • Plotter — draws vector graphics/designs on paper using a pen; common in engineering/CAD drawings.

Speakers and Headphones

Let us hear sound from the computer; a speaker lets everyone in the room listen, a headphone lets only one person listen.

Fact to knowSoftware (the instructions/programs that run on hardware) is divided into System Software and Application Software. Liveware is another name for the computer user (also called "humanware").

STD 8 · CHAPTER 1

Computer Networking

History of networking, network hardware, types of networks, and network topology.

Lesson Plan History Network Hardware Types of Network Network Topology Quick Check Exercise Solutions Extra MCQs Oral Viva Activity Zone Homework Blackboard Revision Notes

Quick Lesson Plan

Duration: 40 minutes
Topics: History of Networking · Network Hardware · Types of Networks · Network Topology

Objectives: students should be able to define a computer network, name the basic networking devices, differentiate PAN/LAN/MAN/WAN, and identify common network topologies.

Hook / Introduction (5 min)

Ask: "How does a WhatsApp message reach your friend's phone instantly?" Let students answer ("the Internet"), then explain: "A network is what connects computers and devices together so they can share information — the Internet is just one giant example."

What is a Computer Network? & History

A network is a group of two or more devices that can communicate with each other — connected either by cables (physical) or wirelessly.

  • In the late 1950s, the US military built one of the earliest computer networks called SAGE (Semi-Automatic Ground Environment).
  • Around the same time, the airline industry built SABRE (Semi-Automatic Business Research Environment) for flight reservations.
  • In 1969, the US Department of Defence created ARPANET (Advanced Research Projects Agency Network), based on designs from the 1960s — this network eventually grew into today's Internet.

Basic Network Hardware

  • NIC (Network Interface Card) — lets a computer join a network; every NIC has a unique MAC address to identify the computer.
  • Repeater — boosts a weak signal and filters out noise.
  • Hub — has multiple ports and simply copies and sends data to every device connected to it.
  • Bridge — connects two network segments together.
  • Switch — smartly forwards data only to the correct device, using MAC addresses.
  • Router — connects different networks to each other (e.g., a home network to the Internet).

Types of Networks

By Size / Area

  • PAN (Personal Area Network) — the smallest network, centred around one person's own devices, e.g., a mobile phone connected to wireless earphones via Bluetooth. A wireless version is called WPAN.
  • LAN (Local Area Network) — connects computers and devices within one building or campus, like a school computer lab or an office, usually using Ethernet cables or Wi-Fi (then called WLAN).
  • MAN (Metropolitan Area Network) — formed by joining several LANs together; covers a larger area like an entire city, often using fast fibre-optic cables.
  • WAN (Wide Area Network) — covers a very large geographic area, connecting many LANs and MANs together; the Internet itself is the best example of a WAN. WANs typically use the TCP/IP protocol along with routers, switches, firewalls and modems.

By Purpose

  • SAN (Storage Area Network) — a dedicated high-speed network that lets multiple servers share storage devices, almost as if each server had its own private hard disk.
  • Enterprise Private Network / Intranet — an organisation's own private network that connects computers across all its departments so that no employee or team is isolated.
  • VPN (Virtual Private Network) — creates a safe, encrypted "tunnel" over a public network like the Internet, so remote workers can securely use company resources from anywhere; it usually needs a password or other authentication to connect.

Network Topology

Topology means how the computers (nodes) in a network are arranged and connected.

  • Star — every workstation connects to one central computer/server (most common topology).
  • Ring — workstations are connected in a closed loop; data can pass through one or more computers to reach its destination.
  • Bus (or Line) — every workstation connects to a single shared cable called the bus.
  • Mesh — workstations are connected directly to many (or all) other workstations, giving multiple possible paths for data.
  • Tree — several star networks are linked together through a main bus, like branches of a tree.
  • Fully Connected — every single workstation is directly connected to every other workstation.

Oral Viva Questions

Give one real-life example of a PAN.

Answer

A phone connected to Bluetooth earphones.

Which network came first historically — ARPANET or SAGE?

Answer

SAGE (late 1950s); ARPANET came later, in 1969.

What is the difference between a hub and a switch?

Answer

A hub sends data to all devices; a switch sends data only to the correct device.

Why would a company use a VPN?

Answer

To let remote employees securely access company resources over the Internet.
